The Case of Missing Sex-Disaggregated COVID-19 Data in India and Why it Matters

By Fazil Khan

Representative Image. Source: Wikimedia Commons

Women in India have a higher relative risk of COVID-19 mortality than men despite having a lower overall caseload. This is contrary to the global trend where most countries with available data show a higher fatality rate amongst males compared to females.

A study published in the Journal of Global Health Science in June last year estimated that the women in India had a higher COVID-19 case fatality rate for women in India was 3.3 percent, compared to 2.9 for men. Simply put, it means that more women were dying compared to men for the same number of reported cases. Case fatality rate (CFR) is the proportion of deaths from the coronavirus (SARS-CoV2) infection out of the total number of cases.

A global database called "The Sex, Gender and COVID-19 Project", which collects gender-disaggregated data on deaths caused by the coronavirus from official government websites, found a similar trend albeit with a slight difference in actual CFR figures.

While these figures raise an alarm, what's worse is that both of these analyses are based on data that is almost a year old.

Currently, in the midst of a second and far worse wave of infections, India had last published sex-disaggregated data on COVID-19 deaths in May last year. This was when the numbers were relatively lower and the country had not yet witnessed its first surge of infections as was the case with the United States or in Europe. Similar data for cases was last published in September.

Amongst the nations burdened with the highest overall caseload, India is only better placed compared to Russia as far as publishing sex-disaggregated data COVID-19 is concerned. Russia has never published such data.

"These countries [Russia, India, Brazil, and Turkey] account for 59 percent of the cases with unknown sex and 48 percent of deaths with unknown sex," the latest update of the "The Sex, Gender, and COVID-19 Project said.

In fact, "The Sex, Gender and COVID-19 Project" dataset that used official data to calculate the CFR for males and females did so with just under 5,000 cases and just over a hundred deaths for which these demographic details were available. The study published in the Journal of Global Health Science, on the other hand, used crowdsourced data from Covid19India.org. This crowdsourced data now has huge gaps given the inconsistency of available data from official bulletins across states.

The Ministry of Health and Family Welfare did not respond to requests for comment.

The initial findings on CFR for women in India had come as a surprise to researchers since biological factors such as a stronger immune response and behavioral risk factors like relatively higher mobility, smoking, and other lifestyle habits place men at a greater risk for health complications and death as a consequence of COVID-19.

"It does not make sense," said Anita Raj, a professor at the departments of Medicine and Education Studies, and the director of the Center on Gender Equity and Health at the University of California San Diego. "I am inclined to believe that this is not due to a unique physiological phenomenon in the context of India but rather something that must be social in terms of either the capture or the management of infection."

There could be a few social reasons that could be behind this trend. One, a sex difference can be seen in the likelihood of bringing someone in for medical care amongst older people, who are at a greater risk of falling severely ill in the case of an infection. This is the case since there's an investment in medical costs involved. So, "What we're seeing is by the time that there's a hospitalization [in the case of women] it may be [at a] later stage," Raj said.

Second, similar to many countries the healthcare system in India is predominantly supported, particularly on the front lines of care, by women. Therefore, exposures to the virus in the healthcare system could be different for men and women. Women are also disproportionately burdened with the expectation to perform informal caregiving in households, and the likelihood of shielding oneself when a household is exposed to infection is limited for women.

The lack of sex-disaggregated makes it difficult for deeper research into the causes of a higher CFR for women in India, if it has persisted to be higher even after May 2020, and if women are, indeed, at a relatively higher risk of mortality from COVID-19 in India.

"If you don't have the data, you can't build a data-driven response and a data-driven response will be more effective in managing a pandemic," Raj said. She added that while she doesn't think there would be meaningful higher mortality but if it is the case, then there is some different physiologic phenomenon that is occurring in India that we need to understand better. This is only possible with more data.
